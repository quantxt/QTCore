Bank of England cuts growth forecast and warns real wages will fall


Bank of England Governor Mark Carney on Thursday said that a consumer slowdown in the British economy has now started in earnest, as rising inflation officially starts to hit regular Brits.

The Bank said this year would be the worst for the pressure on household finances, with wage growth set to pick up over the next three years, while the wider economy will also strengthen. The BoE policy decision was accompanied by the release of the policy statement which supported the view that, on the assumption of a "smooth" transition to Brexit, monetary policy will likely tighten by a somewhat greater extent than the market is now pricing in.

The MPC expects growth to remain at current rates for the rest of the year, blaming the slowdown on consumer-facing sectors, partly reflecting the effect of sterling's depreciation on spending.

Laying out the BoE's definition of a "smooth Brexit", Mr Carney said this entailed an agreement on a future European Union trading arrangement and a transition deal as the United Kingdom extricates itself from the bloc.

The Bank has cut its GDP growth forecast in the short term but raised its projections in the medium term, predicting growth of 1.7% in 2018 and 1.8% in 2019.

"The Bank of England is stuck between a rock and hard place", said Aberdeen Asset Management Chief Economist Lucy O'Carroll.

Inflation in March was at 2.3%, according to the Office for National Statistics, above the bank's target of 2%. With inflation forecast to rise higher than wage growth, real incomes could face a significant squeeze in 2017.
